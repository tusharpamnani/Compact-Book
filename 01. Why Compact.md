# Why Compact?

This document explores why Compact exists, what problem it solves, and how it compares to the alternative approaches to building privacy-preserving smart contracts.

> Source: [docs.midnight.network/what-is-midnight](https://docs.midnight.network/what-is-midnight), [docs.midnight.network/compact](https://docs.midnight.network/compact)

---

## Understanding the Landscape

- **Raw ZK circuit development:** Writing zero-knowledge proofs manually requires deep cryptographic expertise — circuit design, proof system knowledge, constraint system modeling. This is inaccessible to most application developers.
- **Traditional smart contracts (Solidity, etc.):** Everything is on-chain and visible. There is no notion of private state. All balances, actions, and metadata are exposed on a shared public ledger.
- **Compact:** A TypeScript-like language that compiles automatically to ZK circuits. Developers write familiar structured code; the compiler handles the cryptography.

---

## Factors to Consider

### 1. Accessibility

- **Raw ZK development:** Requires cryptographic expertise. You need to understand how constraint systems work, how to design circuits, and how the proving system operates.
- **Traditional smart contracts:** Accessible to developers, but offer no privacy primitives. Everything is public by default.
- **Compact:** Designed for mainstream developers. The official docs describe it as making "privacy-preserving smart contracts accessible to mainstream developers." You write TypeScript-like syntax; the compiler produces proving circuits automatically.

### 2. Privacy Model

- **Traditional blockchains:** Public chains reveal balances, actions, and metadata on a shared ledger. Private chains improve confidentiality but reduce decentralization.
- **Compact on Midnight:** Maintains two parallel states — a public ledger and private local state. Compact lets you write contracts that operate on private data and only surface ZK proofs on-chain. Validators verify correctness without ever seeing the underlying inputs.

### 3. Type Safety

- **JavaScript/Solidity:** Dynamically typed or loosely typed. Type errors surface at runtime.
- **Compact:** Strongly and statically typed. The compiler rejects any program that doesn't type-check. There are no missing type declarations and no unsafe casts. Runtime checks are also generated to enforce types at the boundary with TypeScript.

### 4. Verifiability vs. Privacy

- **Traditional systems:** You get verifiability (everything is on-chain and auditable) at the cost of privacy.
- **ZK systems without a high-level language:** You can get privacy, but writing and auditing raw circuits is extremely difficult and error-prone.
- **Compact:** Private computation happens locally. A ZK proof of correctness is submitted on-chain. Validators verify the proof in milliseconds. You get both privacy and verifiability without writing a single circuit by hand.

### 5. Compliance and Selective Disclosure

- **Public chains:** Cannot comply with frameworks like GDPR, CCPA, or HIPAA because all data is visible by default.
- **Compact:** Supports selective disclosure — users choose exactly what information to reveal and to whom. For regulated use cases, Midnight provides optional compliance mechanisms that allow required reporting without exposing data to unauthorized parties.

---

## When to Choose Compact

- When you need **privacy-preserving logic** on a public blockchain
- When you want to **prove correctness** of a computation without revealing the inputs
- When you are a **TypeScript developer** who does not want to learn ZK circuit design
- When your use case involves **regulated data** — healthcare, finance, identity, governance
- When you need **selective disclosure** — proving a fact without revealing the underlying data

## When Compact May Not Be What You Need

- When your application has **no privacy requirements** — a fully public contract on a traditional chain may be simpler
- When you need **Turing-complete computation** — Compact is intentionally bounded; all loops and types must have sizes fixed at compile time
- When you need **dynamic, runtime-sized data structures** — everything in Compact has a compile-time-known size

---

## The Core Tradeoff

Compact trades away Turing-completeness and dynamic typing in exchange for automatic ZK circuit compilation, strong type safety, and a privacy model that works at the language level rather than requiring it to be bolted on afterward.

That is not a limitation to work around. It is the design.
